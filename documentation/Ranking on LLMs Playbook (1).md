# **How to Write Content That Ranks**

# **on LLMs, AI Overviews, ChatGPT & Perplexity**

Google ranks pages. AI engines rank chunks.

A chunk is a 200–500 word section of your page.  one H2 block and its supporting paragraphs. When ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Google AI Overviews retrieve content to build an answer, they do not read your article. They retrieve the chunks that most directly match the sub-queries they are solving. Every H2 section on your page is a separate citation candidate, evaluated independently.

This changes everything about how to write. You are no longer writing a single document that earns a ranking. You are writing a collection of self-contained answer blocks, each of which competes on its own to be retrieved. A weak section cannot hide behind a strong How to Write Content That Ranks on LLMs, AI Overviews, ChatGPT & Perplexity

| The RAG process in plain English (why this matters) 1\. A user asks ChatGPT a question. 2\. ChatGPT breaks that question into 3–5 sub-queries and searches the web (via Bing) for each one. 3\. It retrieves the most relevant 200–500 word chunks from multiple pages. 4\. It synthesises those chunks into a single answer and cites the sources. What this means for your writing: → Your H2 section title is your chunk's search query match. It must be a specific question. → The first sentence of each section is the most-retrieved text. It must answer the heading directly. → Content that depends on previous sections for context will be skipped  chunks are evaluated in isolation. → You are optimising for chunk rank, not page rank. Every section stands alone. |

## **Part 1  How to Structure a Blog Post for AI Citation**

Structure is the highest-leverage decision you make. According to Kevin Indig and AirOps' analysis of 1.2 million ChatGPT citations, 44.2% of all citations came from the first 30% of a page's content. AirOps found that pages cited by ChatGPT averaged 13.75 list sections per page, more than 17 times higher than typical page-one Google results.

## **The Anatomy of a Citation-Ready Article**

Every AI-optimised article should follow this structure, in this order:

### **1\. Title: Frame the Question, not the Topic**

Your H1 must be the exact question a user would type into ChatGPT. This is the first entity-echo opportunity. 78.4% of ChatGPT citations with questions come from headings. The H1 is the master heading that all H2s serve.

* **Bad: 'Variable Sales Compensation: A Complete Guide' ✗**

* **Bad: 'The Ultimate Guide to LLM Content Optimisation' ✗**

* **Good: 'What Is Variable Sales Compensation and How Does It Work?' ✓**

* **Good: 'How to Write Content That Gets Cited by ChatGPT in 2026' ✓**

### **2\. TL;DR Block \- 40-60 words, directly under the H1** 

Write 1 opening sentence (under 25 words) that summarises the entire article's scope, plus 2–4 bullet points.

**Rules:** Plain active voice. Starts with the focus keyword. NOT the same as your meta description.

**Why:** This is the first chunk retrieved for broad queries. AI Overviews open with a direct fact or answer 99.8% of the time ([SearchIntel, 2025](https://www.searchintel.tech/blog/how-to-appear-in-google-ai-overviews/)). If your first 200 words are scene-setting prose, you lose the broadest queries.

### **3\. Opening Paragraph \- The BLUF Section (200 Words Max)**

44.2% of all citations come from the first 30% of a page. The first 200 words form your highest-density citation opportunity. These words must do three things in exactly this order:

* **Sentence 1–2:** Direct answer to the title query. The primary keyword appears in sentence 1  entity echoing.

* **Sentence 3–4**: One named, dated, linked statistic that verifies or contextualises the claim.

* **Sentence 5–6:** One sentence of context (who this applies to, when, what the stakes are).

* Under 200 words total. Everything else is expansion that comes after.

| First 200 words  before and after BEFORE (gets skipped by AI):  'In today's digital-first world, content strategy has become more important than ever. Brands across industries are rethinking how they approach their blog and content calendar. This article will explore variable sales compensation in depth, covering everything you need to know.' AFTER (gets cited by AI):  'Variable sales compensation is pay that changes based on performance, typically combining a fixed base salary with commission, bonuses, or accelerators tied to quota attainment. According to Bridge Group's 2025 SaaS Benchmarks, 78% of SaaS companies use a 70/30 base-to-variable split as their baseline structure. This guide covers how each component works, how to set the right split for your role type, and what the data says about which structures drive the highest quota attainment.' |
| :---- |

**Why:** The first chunk is matched to the highest-volume queries. Vague openings fail the first retrieval test.

### **4\. H2 Headings** 

78.4% of citations with questions come from headings. Your H2 is AI's user prompt. The paragraph below it is the generated response. Write every H2 as a question. Use entity echoing: the H2 asks about X, the first word of the paragraph is X.

* **Bad: 'Overview of Variable Comp Structures' ✗** 

  * **Good: 'What Are the Main Variable Comp Structures?' ✓**

* **Bad: 'Benefits and Drawbacks' ✗** 

  *  **Good: 'What Are the Pros and Cons of Variable Sales Compensation?' ✓**

* **Bad: 'Best Practices' ✗** 

  *  **Good: 'What Does Best-in-Class Variable Comp Look Like?' ✓**

* Entity echoing: H2 says 'What Are the Main Variable Comp Structures?' → paragraph opens: 'Variable comp structures fall into four types: commission, bonus, accelerators, and SPIFs.'

Two things must happen together: (1) the H2 is a question, and (2) the first word of the paragraph immediately following it is the main entity from that question.

| Entity echoing  the pattern in full H2: 'What is Everstage used for?' First word of paragraph: 'Everstage is a sales compensation platform that...' H2: 'How does variable sales compensation affect quota attainment?' First word of paragraph: 'Variable sales compensation increases quota attainment by...' The H2 asks about the entity. The paragraph opens by naming the entity immediately. Never open with 'It', 'This', 'The', or 'There are'. Open with the entity itself. |
| :---- |

**Why:** LLMs select chunks that match the natural phrasing of user queries (SMX Advanced 2025). Question headings are already sub-query matches. 

### **5\. Answer Capsule** 

Content with answer capsules after H2 headings was cited 67% more frequently than content without them. The capsule is the standalone extractable statement that directly answers the H2 question. No links inside it.

* **Format:** 120–150 characters. Zero internal links. Complete, standalone statement. Can be read without any surrounding context.

* **Bad capsule:** 'In this section we cover the main types of variable compensation structures used in SaaS companies today.'

* **Good capsule**: 'Variable comp structures include commission (% of revenue), bonuses (milestone-based), and accelerators (above-quota multipliers).'

* Write the capsule before you write the section body. If you cannot write a clear standalone answer in 150 characters, you do not understand the topic well enough to write the section.

### **6\. Section Body**

RAG systems retrieve chunks in isolation. A section that says 'as we covered above' or 'building on the previous point' fails when that chunk is the only one retrieved. Every H2 section must be independently intelligible.

* Open with the answer capsule (sentence 1 of the section body)

* One supporting paragraph: 2–3 sentences, one named stat minimum

* A table, numbered list, or definition block (the most-extracted formats)

* End with a 40–60 word 'In short:' micro-summary  a second extraction target

* Word count check: under 200 \= not enough for a clean chunk. Over 400 \= split into two H2 sections.

* Define any acronym the first time it appears in each section, even if defined earlier

* Name your subject on every reference. Not 'the platform'  'Salesforce'. Not 'this approach'  'variable compensation'

###  **7\. Comparison Tables for every X vs Y or Multi-option topic**

Whenever your content covers multiple options, choices, or comparisons  use a table.

* Keep tables to 3–5 rows maximum for highest citation probability

* Use clear descriptive column headers, not just 'Option A / Option B'

* Add a descriptive heading above every table naming the subject and data type

Why: Tables are one of the most-extracted formats in Google AI Overviews and reduce ambiguity in RAG retrieval.

### **8\. Conclusion Section**

24.7% of all citations come from the final 30% of a page. AI skips the footer but 'wakes up' at the conclusion. The conclusion must be 5–7 standalone bullet points  not a paragraph summary. Each bullet must work as an isolated takeaway without the surrounding article.

* **Bad conclusion:** **'In summary, variable sales compensation is a flexible approach that companies should consider when designing their compensation strategy, keeping in mind their specific circumstances.'**

* **Good conclusion  5 standalone bullets, each independently citable:**

  * **Variable sales compensation links rep pay to performance. The 70/30 split is the SaaS benchmark.**

  * **Enterprise AEs carry higher variable ratios (40–50%) due to longer deal cycles.**

  * **Quota attainment improves by 19% on average when variable comp is properly calibrated (Bridge Group, 2025).**

  * **Avoid salary compression  uncapped plans with accelerators retain high performers while managing base payroll.**

  * **Review your comp plan quarterly. AI engines deprioritise pages older than 90 days without updates.**

###  **9\. FAQ Section \- 5-8 questions, 40-60 word answers each**

Place FAQs near the end of the article. 

**Source questions from:** People Also Ask on Google, AlsoAsked.com, and AnswerThePublic.

Each question must be phrased as a natural search query. Each answer must be 40–60 words and standalone.

* Add FAQPage schema to every article with this section

**Why:** Pages with FAQPage schema achieve a 41% citation rate vs 15% without it (Relixir, 2025). FAQ sections are sub-query extraction targets.  

## **Part 2  The 12 Writing Rules, One by One**

These apply at sentence level. Apply them during drafting, not editing  they change how you write, not how you fix.

### **Rule 1: Write sentences under 20 words**

Cited content reads at Flesch-Kincaid grade 16 (college level). Uncited content averages grade 19 (academic/PhD). AI extracts most reliably from subject-verb-object sentences with specific, single-clause meaning.

| Too long (AI skips it) 'The implementation of variable sales compensation structures across enterprise SaaS organisations requires careful calibration of the relationship between base salary components and performance-linked variable elements to ensure alignment with both individual and organisational objectives.' | Rewritten (AI cites this) 'Enterprise SaaS companies use variable sales compensation to link rep pay to quota performance. The optimal base-to-variable split depends on role seniority and deal cycle length.' |
| :---- | :---- |

| The sentence-splitting rule If your sentence has more than one clause or runs over 20 words  split it.   Before: 'The implementation of variable sales compensation structures across enterprise SaaS organisations requires careful calibration of the relationship between base salary and performance-linked elements to ensure alignment with organisational objectives.' After (three sentences, each citable independently):   'Enterprise SaaS companies use variable sales compensation to link rep pay to quota performance.'   'The calibration challenge is the base-to-variable split  typically 70/30 for mid-market AEs.'   'Misaligned splits are the leading cause of rep underperformance in the first 6 months (Bridge Group, 2025).' |
| :---- |

**Why:** LLM systems understand clear, direct sentences best. Metaphors, jokes, and digressions reduce semantic analysis quality. Sentences over 20 words embed meaning in context that RAG systems cannot reliably extract in isolation.

### **Rule 2: Never use vague attribution  name the source, every time**

| Invisible to AI 'Research shows that variable pay increases performance.' 'Studies indicate that commission structures drive revenue.' 'Experts agree that incentive design matters.' | Citable by AI 'According to Bridge Group's 2025 SaaS Benchmarks report, variable pay increases quota attainment by 19% on average.' Source: \[link to primary study\] |
| :---- | :---- |

**Why:** Named, attributed statistics with links increased citation rates by 41% in Atlas Marketing's 50-article test. AI engines distinguish between sites that generate data and sites that reference data. Vague attribution fails the verifiability check.

### **Rule 3: Keep your first chunk to 200 words  make it the best answer in the index**

Your opening 200 words form your first retrieved chunk  the one matched to the broadest, highest-volume queries. If those 200 words are an intro, a definition of why the topic matters, or a promise of what you'll cover, the RAG system skips you for a competitor's more direct opening.

| What a first chunk must contain (all three) 1\. Direct answer to the title query in sentence 1  your primary keyword in sentence 1\. 2\. One named, dated statistic that verifies the claim. 3\. One sentence of context explaining who this matters for. Total: under 200 words. Everything else comes after. |
| :---- |

### **Rule 4: Avoid vague time language in the first 200 words especially**

| AI deprioritises this 'Recently, companies have been shifting toward...' 'In recent years, this trend has accelerated...' 'Nowadays, the landscape looks quite different...' | AI trusts this 'As of Q1 2025, 78% of SaaS companies use variable comp (Everstage Benchmark, 2025).' 'Since January 2024, Google AI Overviews have appeared in 88% of informational queries (Semrush, 2025).' |
| :---- | :---- |

 **Why:** AI models treat undated claims as lower-confidence. Pages not updated at least quarterly are 3x more likely to lose AI citations over time (Search Engine Land, 2025).

### **Rule 5: Write for entity clarity  name everything explicitly every time**

LLMs build a knowledge graph of entities: people, products, companies, concepts. When content uses vague language, it fails entity mapping and gets deprioritised for related queries.

| Vague (entity-blind) | Specific (entity-clear) |
| :---- | :---- |
| **'The platform recently announced...'** | 'Salesforce announced in February 2025 that...' |
| **'This tool helps with X...'** | 'Everstage, the sales compensation platform, tracks...' |
| **'Our methodology...'** | 'RevvGrowth's 8-Step AEO Workflow...' |
| **'Industry leaders agree...'** | 'Gartner's 2025 Market Guide states...' |
| **'Recent research shows...'** | 'According to Bridge Group's 2025 SaaS Report...' |

### **Rule 6: One original data point per piece  minimum**

LLMs have read the 500th generic guide on your topic. What makes your content citable is information that does not exist anywhere else. Three types of original data that are consistently cited:

* **Original testing:** 'We tested this across 50 articles and saw 67% improvement'  specific, attributed to your own experience

* **Case study with before/after numbers:** 'Client X went from 550 to 2,300 AI-referred trials in 4 weeks using this structure'

* **Named proprietary framework:** Create a name for your process ('The BLUF-First Method')  named frameworks become entities AI can attribute

**Why:** Content based on original research has an outsized citation advantage. Ekamoira's citation research confirms: original research stands out because the information is not widely duplicated. 

### **Rule 7: Q\&A is the best format for GEO  Chris Green's June 2025 experiment**

SEO practitioner Chris Green ran a direct experiment on content format and AI inclusion. Finding: Q\&A format delivered the highest semantic relevance to user queries. Structured content with headings and lists was nearly as effective. Dense prose was the worst format for AI inclusion.

| The practical ranking of formats (best → worst for AI citation) 1st  Q\&A: Question heading followed immediately by a direct 2–3 sentence answer 2nd  Structured with headings and lists: Question H2 \+ bullet or numbered body 3rd  Definition block: 'X is \[definition\]. Key characteristics are: \[list\]' 4th  Numbered step-by-step: Sequential process with numbered items Last  Dense prose: Long paragraphs without structural breaks |
| :---- |

### **Rule 8: Write sections that answer the adjacent questions too**

When ChatGPT handles a query, it fans out into 3–5 sub-queries. Google confirmed use of this 'query fan-out' technique in AI Overviews. A page that answers only the primary query gets cited once. A page that answers the primary query and 4 adjacent ones gets cited multiple times for the same topic.

| How to find adjacent questions for every piece Type the H2 question into ChatGPT in a new session. Note the follow-up questions it generates. Check AlsoAsked.com for the second and third layer questions on your topic. Look at 'People Also Ask' on Google for your primary keyword. Scroll to the bottom of any Perplexity answer for 'Related Questions'. Add the top 4 adjacent questions as H3 sub-sections or FAQ entries. These become additional chunk citation targets. |

#### **Fan-Out: The Hidden Citation Surface Your Competitors Are Missing**

The most actionable finding from the AirOps study of 548,534 pages: ChatGPT does not search just your primary query. It fans out into 3–5 follow-up searches for almost every single prompt.

#### **What fan-out is and why it matters**

When ChatGPT receives a query, it generates internal follow-up searches  fan-out queries  to research the full answer. These are not visible to users. They are internal sub-searches that expand the citation surface far beyond the original keyword.

| 89.6% of ChatGPT searches generate 2+ fan-out queries *AirOps, 15,000 prompts* | 32.9% of cited pages appeared only in fan-out SERP results *AirOps, 43,233 queries* | 95% of fan-out queries have zero traditional search volume *AirOps research* |
| :---: | :---: | :---: |

 95% of ChatGPT's fan-out queries have zero monthly search volume by traditional keyword metrics. This means the entire traditional keyword research workflow misses nearly all of the fan-out citation surface. You cannot find these queries in Ahrefs or SEMrush  you have to generate them yourself. 

#### **How fan-out behaves by query type**

| Query type | Fan-out behaviour | What to write to capture it |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Definition queries** | Stay near-verbatim 51.6% of the time. Low fan-out rewrite. | One page can answer the primary \+ most fan-outs. Build comprehensive definition pages. |
| **How-to queries** | Stay near-verbatim 42.6%. When rewritten: add category or tool context. | Include tool-specific and scenario-specific sections. 'How to do X with Salesforce' as an H3. |
| **Evaluation queries** | Split into sub-queries 37.5% of the time: benefits, limitations, use cases. | Explicitly add a Limitations or When Not To Use section. This is where Claude also cites more. |
| **Comparison queries** | Split into sub-queries 38.4%  highest of any type. A HubSpot vs Salesforce query becomes separate searches for pricing, features, and reviews. | Build modular comparison pages. Each major dimension (pricing, features, use case fit) as its own H2. |
| **Research / 'best' queries** | Near-verbatim 50.6% but adds year modifiers 9.7% of the time. | Include the year in your title or first paragraph. Refresh annually. 'Best X in 2026' outperforms 'Best X.' |

#### **How to generate fan-out queries for your content (the practical method)**

Since fan-out queries are internal to ChatGPT and invisible, you have to reverse-engineer them. Here is the method:

| Fan-out method  Generate the hidden sub-queries before writing Open ChatGPT in a fresh incognito session. Type your primary H1 question. After it generates the answer, immediately ask: 'What sub-queries did you search to answer that?' Then ask: 'What follow-up questions would someone researching this topic likely ask next?' Document every distinct sub-query generated. Each sub-query becomes either an H2 section, an H3 sub-section, or an FAQ entry in your article Target: cover 5+ fan-out queries per article. Each one you cover \= one additional citation path ChatGPT can use Also use: AlsoAsked.com, People Also Ask on Google, and Perplexity's 'Related Questions' at the bottom of any answer |
| :---- |

 

| Fan-out example  'What is variable sales compensation?' Primary query: 'What is variable sales compensation?' Likely fan-out queries ChatGPT generates internally:   1\. 'Variable sales compensation definition'  → Your BLUF opening \+ answer capsule   2\. 'Variable pay vs fixed salary difference'  → An H2: 'How Does Variable Comp Differ from Fixed Salary?'   3\. 'Variable compensation SaaS benchmark 2025'  → An H2: 'What Is the Industry Benchmark for Variable Comp Split?'   4\. 'Commission vs bonus vs accelerator'  → An H2: 'What Are the Main Types of Variable Compensation?'   5\. 'Best variable comp tools for SaaS'  → An H2 or FAQ: 'Which Tools Track Variable Compensation?'   Each fan-out query covered \= one more citation path. An article covering all five earns citations for five different user prompts, not one. |

### **Rule 9: Stat density  one attributed stat every 150–200 words**

Frase.io's GEO analysis found that the core principle of effective GEO includes maintaining fact density with statistics every 150–200 words. This is not about stuffing numbers in  it is about providing the AI with verifiable anchor points throughout the document. Each named stat is a chunk quality signal.

* Every stat must have: the number, the source name, the year, and a link

* Stats from primary sources only  do not cite a blog that cites the study, cite the study

* If you cannot find a named source, do not include the claim  vague claims hurt citation rates

### **Rule 10: Short paragraphs  2–3 sentences, one idea each**

LLMs process content in token windows. Breaking content into short, single-idea paragraphs creates more clean extraction points. Semrush's guide specifies: use short paragraphs (2–3 lines max) to reduce cognitive load, and lead each section with 1–2 sentences that answer the heading directly.

| Don't write this 'Variable sales compensation, sometimes referred to as variable pay or incentive compensation, is a method of structuring employee remuneration in which a portion of total earnings is contingent on measurable performance outcomes such as quota attainment, revenue generated, or customer acquisition metrics, and this approach is particularly prevalent in sales-driven organisations where linking compensation to performance outcomes is seen as an effective motivational lever...' \[88 words, one paragraph\] | Write this instead 'Variable sales compensation ties a portion of rep pay to performance outcomes.' 'The most common structures combine a fixed base salary with commission, bonuses, or accelerators based on quota attainment.' 'According to Bridge Group's 2025 report, 78% of SaaS companies use a 70/30 base-to-variable split as their baseline.' |

### **Rule 12: Refresh your content  30-day cycle for top pieces**

This is the most consistently overlooked rule and one of the most impactful. 76.4% of ChatGPT's most-cited pages were updated within the past 30 days (Atlas Marketing, 200-article test). Content older than 90 days without updates loses citation rates by more than half regardless of original quality.

* Set calendar reminders for your top 20 pieces  monthly

* Add one updated stat, one revised example, or one new data point per refresh cycle

* Update the visible 'last updated' date on the page, not just the CMS metadata  AI crawlers read visible dates

* Avoid vague time language ('recently', 'in recent years')  replace with specific dated claims on every refresh

## **Part 5  Writing Patterns That Hurt Citation Rates**

These are tested failures. Do not do these.

| Pattern | Why it fails | Fix |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Keyword stuffing over 2% density** | In testing: natural 0.8% density was cited 4x more than 2.5% density. AI reads meaning, not frequency. | Write naturally. Never repeat a keyword more than once in a paragraph. |
| **Opening with 'In this article...' or 'In today's digital landscape...'** | Your first 200 words form your first chunk. If they're scene-setting, you fail retrieval for the broadest queries. | State the direct answer in sentence 1\. Cut every preamble. |
| **Generic attribution: 'studies show', 'experts agree'** | Vague attribution fails AI verifiability checks. Named, dated statistics increased citations by 41%. | Name the source, year, and specific number every time. Link to primary research. |
| **Sections that reference previous sections** | RAG retrieves chunks in isolation. 'As mentioned above' fails when that section is the only chunk retrieved. | Every H2 section must be independently intelligible. Restate context rather than referencing it. |
| **No links inside the answer capsule is required  links IN the capsule break it** | 90%+ of ChatGPT-cited answer capsules had zero internal links. Links make the capsule less extractable. | Write your 120–150 char answer capsule as a link-free standalone sentence. |
| **Optimising for all engines equally** | ChatGPT wants short capsules. Claude wants long analysis. Opposing preferences cancel out. | Choose a primary engine based on client domain authority. Optimise aggressively for one. Treat others as bonuses. |
| **Publishing without refreshing** | Pages not refreshed quarterly are 3x more likely to lose AI citations. | Set monthly reminders for top 20 pieces. Update one stat, one example, or one claim per cycle. |
| **No visible 'last updated' date** | AI crawlers assess freshness. Content without visible dates is deprioritised for recency-sensitive queries. | Display 'Last updated: \[Month Year\]' visibly on every piece. Not just in metadata. |
| **Dense prose with no structure** | Chris Green's experiment: dense prose was the worst-performing format for AI inclusion. | Break every 200 words into a list, table, Q\&A block, or definition. Prose-only content is the lowest citation format. |
| **Generic AI-generated content with no information gain** | LLMs have read the 500th generic guide. They have no reason to cite content that duplicates what exists. | Every piece must contribute at least one of: original test data, case study with numbers, or named proprietary framework. |

## **Part 7  The Self-Test Before You Hit Publish**

Run these tests on every piece before it goes live. They take 10 minutes total.

| Structural tests (can be done in the doc)   Is the H1 a direct question?   Does sentence 1 of the article directly answer the H1 query?   Is the opening under 200 words?   Does every H2 follow question format?   Is there a 120–150 char answer capsule after every H2, with no links?   Is every answer capsule standalone  makes sense without surrounding context?   Does every section end with a 40–60 word 'In short:' summary?   Is there a comparison table or numbered list in every section where one is possible?   Are there 5–8 FAQ entries with 40–60 word standalone answers?   Does the conclusion use 5–7 standalone bullet points (not a paragraph)?   Is there at least one original data point (test result, case study, or proprietary stat)?   Is every factual claim attributed to a named source, year, and link?   Are all paragraphs 2–3 sentences maximum?   Is the piece 200–400 words per H2 section?   Is the visible 'last updated' date on the page? |

| Live AI test (takes 5 minutes, incognito mode)  Copy the answer capsule under your most important H2. Paste it as a query into ChatGPT. Does it work as a complete, citable answer? If ChatGPT would need more context, rewrite the capsule.   Search your H1 title in ChatGPT and note which domains are currently cited. What structural differences do you see vs your article?   Search your H1 title in Perplexity. Are the cited sources more recent than yours? If yes, ensure your visible date is current and content is fresher.   Search your H1 in Google (incognito). Does an AI Overview appear? If yes, are you cited? If not, compare the cited sources' FAQ sections and schema against yours. |
